Lagrange Points

Five special places where gravity and orbital mechanics conspire to create stability

The three-body problem — simplified

In 1772, Joseph-Louis Lagrange proved that in a system of two massive bodies (like the Sun and Earth), there are exactly 5 points where a small third body can maintain a stable position relative to the other two. These are the Lagrange points L1–L5.

At these points, the gravitational pull of both large bodies, combined with the centrifugal force of orbiting with them, all balance out. A spacecraft placed there needs minimal fuel to stay put.

The five points

L1: Between Earth and Sun, ~1.5 million km sunward. Used by solar observatories (SOHO, DSCOVR) — always facing the Sun.

L2: Beyond Earth, ~1.5 million km anti-sunward. JWST lives here. Also home to Gaia, Euclid, and Planck.

L3: On the opposite side of the Sun from Earth. Largely unused (communication is difficult with the Sun in the way).

L4, L5: 60° ahead of and behind Earth in its orbit. Stable "gravity wells" — Jupiter's L4 and L5 trap thousands of Trojan asteroids.

L2 in everyday terms: Imagine you're on a merry-go-round, and someone is holding a flag on a stick extending 1.5 metres behind you. The flag always stays behind you as you rotate, always in your shadow. That's L2 — it orbits the Sun at the same rate as Earth, always staying "behind" Earth relative to the Sun. It's not orbiting Earth; it's co-orbiting the Sun, locked in formation.

Why L2 is Perfect for JWST

Five reasons L2 is the ideal location

1. Thermal stability: At L2, the Sun, Earth, and Moon are all on the same side of the telescope. The sunshield can block all three simultaneously with a single, fixed orientation. This provides an incredibly stable thermal environment — no cycling between sunlight and shadow every 90 minutes (as in low Earth orbit).

2. Unobstructed view: JWST can observe ~39% of the sky at any given time (the hemisphere facing away from the Sun). Over 6 months, as Earth orbits the Sun, L2 moves too — eventually giving access to the entire sky. No Earth occultations, no atmospheric absorption.

3. Communication: L2 is close enough to Earth (1.5 million km, ~5 light-seconds) for high-bandwidth Ka-band communication. Deep space missions (e.g., Voyager at 24 billion km) can only whisper back data.

4. Low radiation environment: L2 is outside the Van Allen radiation belts that would degrade detectors in low Earth orbit. It's also far enough from Earth's magnetotail to avoid trapped particle events.

5. Low fuel cost: Station-keeping at L2 requires only ~2–4 m/s of velocity correction per year (tiny thruster burns every ~21 days). This is why JWST's fuel will last 20+ years despite carrying only ~150 kg of hydrazine.

Comparison to Hubble: Hubble orbits at 540 km altitude — it spends half of every 97-minute orbit in Earth's shadow, causing thermal cycling of ~200°C every 1.5 hours. Its solar panels degrade from radiation. The atmosphere limits its lowest wavelengths. And it can only observe when Earth isn't in the way (~50% of the time). JWST avoids ALL of these problems by being 2,800× further away. The tradeoff: no repair missions possible.

The halo orbit: not sitting at L2, but dancing around it

JWST doesn't sit exactly at the L2 point — it follows a halo orbit around L2 with a period of ~6 months and an amplitude of ~800,000 km. This might seem odd (why not just park exactly at L2?), but there are two reasons:

1. L2 is unstable: L2 is like a ball balanced on a saddle point — stable in one direction, unstable in another. Without occasional corrections, JWST would drift away. The halo orbit is chosen to minimise these corrections.

2. Avoiding Earth's shadow: If JWST sat exactly at L2, it would occasionally enter Earth's shadow (solar eclipse). This would cut power from the solar panels AND cause a thermal transient that could take weeks to recover from. The halo orbit is specifically designed so that JWST never passes through Earth's shadow cone.

L2 distance from Earth: ~1.5 million km ≈ 1% of Earth-Sun distance
Light travel time: ~5 seconds | Halo orbit period: ~6 months | Amplitude: ~800,000 km

Getting There: The Journey to L2

Launch and transit

JWST launched on Christmas Day, December 25, 2021, aboard an Ariane 5 rocket from Kourou, French Guiana. The journey to L2 took 29 days — during which the telescope performed its entire deployment sequence.

Why Ariane 5? ESA provided the launch as their contribution to the mission. The Ariane 5's payload fairing was large enough for JWST's folded configuration, and its injection accuracy was exceptional — so precise that it saved years of fuel that would have been needed for trajectory corrections. This directly extended JWST's operational lifetime.

Mid-course corrections: Three burns during transit refined the trajectory. Importantly, JWST can only fire thrusters away from the Sun (toward L2) — never back toward Earth. If the Ariane 5 had overshot, there would be no way to return. This is why the launch was designed to slightly undershoot — they could always add speed, never subtract it.

The one-way-valve constraint: JWST's sunshield must always face the Sun. If it fired thrusters sunward (to slow down), the exhaust would contaminate the optical surfaces, and the attitude change would expose cold instruments to sunlight. So trajectory corrections can only push it further from the Sun. It's like a sailboat that can only add speed, never brake. The Ariane 5 launch had to be perfect — too fast, and the telescope is lost forever.

Station-keeping: staying at L2 forever

L2 is dynamically unstable — without corrections, JWST would drift away in ~23 days. Station-keeping thruster burns happen approximately every 21 days, each consuming about 0.27 kg of hydrazine fuel.

Fuel budget: JWST launched with ~150 kg of hydrazine. At the current consumption rate (~4.6 kg/year for station-keeping), fuel will last well beyond 20 years. The original 10-year mission requirement has been dramatically exceeded thanks to the Ariane 5's precision.

Fuel consumption: ~4.6 kg/year | Total fuel: ~150 kg | Projected life: 25–30+ years

Unanswered Questions

  • Could a robotic refuelling mission extend JWST's life beyond its fuel limit? NASA has discussed this but not committed.

  • Are there orbital configurations at L2 that would allow formation-flying (multiple telescopes acting as one)?

L2 as humanity's permanent deep-space observatory platform

L2 is accumulating spacecraft: Planck (2009–2013), Gaia (2013–present), JWST (2022–present), Euclid (2023–present). It may become the default location for all future space observatories.

Imagine a future where L2 hosts a constellation: a UV/optical telescope (HWO), an X-ray observatory, a gravitational wave detector (LISA is at a different location, but concepts exist for L2-based designs), and JWST's successor. All sharing data, coordinating observations, serviced by robotic tugs from cislunar space.

The engineering enables this: L2 offers stable thermal environment, full-sky access, proximity to Earth for communication, and low fuel requirements. The same reasons that make it perfect for JWST make it perfect for everything. The question is not "should we build there?" but "why would we build anywhere else?"

No formal plans exist for an L2 constellation. But the trend of placing every new flagship observatory at L2 suggests this will emerge naturally over the next 20–30 years.
