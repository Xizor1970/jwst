WASP-39b — First Detailed Exoplanet Atmosphere

Four transmission spectra from three JWST instruments revealing the atmospheric composition of hot gas giant WASP-39b (about 700 light-years away). Each bump and dip corresponds to a specific molecule absorbing starlight as it passes through the planet's atmosphere. This single observation detected water (H₂O), carbon dioxide (CO₂), sodium (Na), potassium (K), and sulfur dioxide (SO₂).

Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

How transit spectroscopy works

When a planet passes in front of its star, a thin ring of its atmosphere is backlit by starlight. Different molecules absorb different wavelengths — like a chemical fingerprint. By comparing the star's spectrum with and without the planet in front, JWST can identify what's in the atmosphere.

The signal is incredibly faint: we're detecting differences of ~50–200 parts per million in the star's brightness.

How faint is 50 ppm? Imagine standing in Cape Town and someone in Johannesburg (1,400 km away) holds up a standard LED torch. Now imagine a second person passes a coloured sweet wrapper in front of that torch for a few hours. Transit spectroscopy is detecting which colour the sweet wrapper was — at that distance, through the atmosphere, with the naked eye. Except JWST actually pulls it off.

Key discoveries so far

WASP-39b: First detection of CO₂ in an exoplanet atmosphere. Also found SO₂ — evidence of photochemistry (sunlight driving chemical reactions), showing these atmospheres are dynamic, not static.

K2-18b: A "sub-Neptune" in its star's habitable zone. JWST detected methane (CH₄) and CO₂, with a possible hint of dimethyl sulfide (DMS) — on Earth, DMS is only produced by life (phytoplankton). This remains unconfirmed and hotly debated.

TRAPPIST-1 system: Seven rocky, Earth-sized planets. JWST has been systematically checking each for atmospheres — early results suggest at least some may have lost their atmospheres to stellar radiation.

Why DMS matters: On Earth, dimethyl sulfide is produced almost exclusively by marine phytoplankton — it's what gives the ocean its distinctive "sea smell." Finding it on K2-18b would be like finding tyre tracks on Mars — not proof of a car, but very hard to explain without one. The signal is at the edge of detectability, and the debate is fierce.

Unanswered Questions

  • Is the DMS signal on K2-18b real? If confirmed, it would be the first potential biosignature detected on another world.
  • Can rocky planets around red dwarf stars retain atmospheres, or does stellar activity strip them away?
  • What does "habitable" really mean? JWST is showing us that planetary chemistry is far more diverse than we imagined.

Are we looking for life wrong?

Our biosignature search is fundamentally biased toward Earth-like biochemistry. We look for oxygen, methane, DMS — molecules that Earth's biosphere produces. But what if alien life uses completely different metabolic pathways?

Consider: on Earth, photosynthesis evolved to use visible light because that's where our sun's peak emission is. But red dwarf stars (which host most known habitable-zone planets, including TRAPPIST-1 and K2-18b) peak in the near-infrared. Photosynthesis there might use different pigments, producing different waste gases. We might be looking for the wrong molecules entirely.

JWST's strength is that it measures whatever is there — not just what we expect. The spectra are agnostic. If something unexpected shows up (a molecule that has no known abiotic production pathway in those conditions), it could be as significant as DMS — even if it's not in our current "biosignature catalogue."

This reflects an active philosophical debate in astrobiology. The "agnostic biosignature" framework (looking for thermodynamic disequilibrium rather than specific molecules) is gaining traction but is much harder to apply in practice. We're still learning what questions to ask.
