Why Materials Matter

Every material on JWST operates under conditions that would destroy ordinary engineering alloys

The environmental extremes

JWST's materials must survive — simultaneously and for 20+ years:

Temperature range: +85°C on the sun-facing side to -266°C (7 K) at the MIRI detector. That's a 350°C gradient across one structure. Most materials would shatter, warp, or delaminate.

Vacuum: Hard vacuum (< 10⁻⁷ mbar). Materials must not outgas — any molecule that escapes can condense on cold optical surfaces and degrade performance. Even fingerprints left during assembly could sublimate and contaminate mirrors years later.

Radiation: Continuous bombardment by cosmic rays, solar protons, and UV. Polymers degrade. Semiconductors develop defects. Coatings can sputter away.

Dimensional stability: The mirror must hold its shape to 150 nm (the size of a virus) over 20 years of thermal cycling and micrometeorite impacts.

The contamination paranoia: During assembly, the clean room air was filtered to Class 10,000 (fewer than 10,000 particles per cubic foot >0.5 μm). But even that wasn't enough for the mirrors — they had dedicated ISO Class 5 enclosures. A single flake of skin, a molecule of perfume, or a fingerprint oil residue could, over years of UV exposure in vacuum, create a permanent stain on the gold coating. Workers underwent full gowning protocols and no cosmetics were permitted within 50 metres of the hardware.

The Primary Mirror: Beryllium

A toxic, lightweight metal with magical cryogenic properties

Why beryllium?

The mirror segments are made of Brush Wellman O-30 optical-grade beryllium — a powder metallurgy product with controlled microstructure. Beryllium was chosen for three extraordinary properties:

1. Lightweight: Density of 1.85 g/cm³ — about ⅓ of aluminium, ⅙ of steel. JWST's 6.5 m mirror weighs just 705 kg total. A comparable glass mirror (like Hubble's) would weigh ~6,000 kg — unflyable on any current rocket at L2.

2. Stiffness: Elastic modulus of 287 GPa — stiffer than steel (200 GPa) at ⅓ the weight. The specific stiffness (E/ρ) is about 6× steel's. This means the mirror segments don't sag under their own weight (relevant during ground testing, where gravity distorts the shape).

3. Cryogenic dimensional stability: This is the killer property. At temperatures below ~80 K, beryllium's coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) drops to essentially zero. The mirror was figured (polished to its final shape) while cold — and once it reaches operating temperature at L2, it stops changing size. Period.

CTE of Be at 40 K: ~0.02 × 10⁻⁶ /K (vs ~23 × 10⁻⁶ /K for aluminium at room temp)
CTE comparison: At JWST's operating temperature, if the entire 1.32-metre mirror segment experienced a 1-degree temperature fluctuation, it would change size by about 26 nanometres — less than the width of a single virus. At the same temperature change, an aluminium mirror would move ~30,000 nm (30 micrometres) — completely ruining the image. Beryllium essentially "freezes" dimensionally at cryogenic temperatures.

How the mirrors were made

Step 1 — Powder metallurgy: O-30 beryllium powder (99.0% Be, grain size ~10–15 μm) is hot isostatically pressed (HIP'd) at ~1,000°C and 100 MPa into a near-net-shape blank. This produces an extremely fine, uniform grain structure critical for polishability.

Step 2 — Lightweighting: Each blank starts at ~250 kg. The back is machined into a triangular rib structure, reducing mass by ~92% to just ~21 kg per segment. The mirror face sheet is only ~2–3 mm thick, supported by the rib structure. Total weight of all 18 segments: ~705 kg.

Step 3 — Cryogenic figuring: The mirror is polished at room temperature to an intentionally "wrong" shape. Then it's cooled to 25 K in NASA Marshall's X-ray & Cryogenic Facility and measured interferometrically. The difference between the cold shape and the desired shape guides further polishing. This cycle repeats until the cold shape converges to the target — within 25 nm RMS surface error (surface roughness <6 nm RMS).

Step 4 — Gold coating: Once the shape is final, a ~2–3 nm chromium adhesion layer is deposited, followed by 100 nm of gold via vacuum vapour deposition (by Quantum Coating Inc.). Total gold per segment: ~48.25 g. Uniformity requirement: <5% thickness variation across the full 1.32 m segment.

The figuring challenge: You must polish a 1.3-metre mirror to nanometre precision — but not for the shape it needs at room temperature. You need to predict what shape it will relax into when cooled by 270°C, and polish the warm mirror so that the cold mirror ends up correct. It's like cutting a suit for someone — but they'll only wear it while 30% smaller. You have to cut it "wrong" so the shrinkage makes it "right."

The Gold Coating

1,000 atoms thick and worth every one of them

Why gold, not silver or aluminium?

Three options were considered for the reflective coating:

Aluminium: Best in UV/visible, ~88% in IR. But it requires a protective overcoat (oxidises rapidly), and that overcoat adds absorption in the IR. Used on Hubble (which works in visible/UV) but not optimal for JWST's infrared mission.

Silver: ~98% reflective across 1–25 μm. Slightly better than gold in parts of the range. But silver tarnishes — even in vacuum, trace sulfur compounds can corrode it over 20 years. Unacceptable for a non-serviceable mission.

Gold: ~98–99% reflective from 0.7 μm (deep red) through the entire infrared range. Chemically inert — will not corrode, oxidise, or degrade in vacuum over any timescale. The only penalty: poor reflectivity below 0.6 μm (blue/green), which is why the mirrors appear gold to the eye. Since JWST only observes at >0.6 μm, this doesn't matter.

Application: Physical vapour deposition (PVD) in a vacuum chamber. Gold atoms are evaporated from a source and condense on the rotating mirror surface. Total gold per mirror: ~48.25 g (about $3,500 worth at current prices — trivial compared to the $10 billion mission).

Gold coating thickness: 100 nm = ~1,000 atomic layers
Total gold across all 18 segments + secondary: ~705 g
How thin is 100 nm? Stack 700 gold coatings on top of each other and you'd have the thickness of a single sheet of printer paper. The coating is so thin that if you scraped all the gold off the entire telescope, you'd have about the mass of a golf ball. Yet this tissue-thin layer reflects >98% of the infrared light that has travelled 13 billion years to reach it.

The Sunshield: Kapton Polyimide

Five layers of plastic between the telescope and the Sun

What is Kapton?

Kapton is DuPont's trade name for a polyimide film. Its chemical backbone is a chain of aromatic imide rings — giving it extraordinary thermal stability. It doesn't melt; it doesn't burn; it barely outgasses in vacuum. It remains flexible at cryogenic temperatures (where most polymers become brittle glass). And it can be made into films as thin as 25 μm.

JWST's sunshield uses 5 layers of Kapton, with varying thickness:

• Layer 1 (sun-facing): ~50 μm thick Kapton, coated with silicon on the sun side and aluminium on the space side.

• Layers 2–4: ~25 μm thick Kapton, aluminium-coated on both sides.

• Layer 5 (cold side): ~25 μm thick Kapton, aluminium-coated on both sides.

Why five layers and not one thick blanket?

Each layer blocks most radiation — but it also re-radiates heat from its own surface. A single layer would reach thermal equilibrium at some intermediate temperature and radiate inward.

With separated layers in vacuum (gaps of 20–30 cm maintained by spreader bars), each successive layer only receives radiation from the layer before it — and each re-radiates half of that outward into space. The temperature drops dramatically at each gap:

• Layer 1 (sun-facing): ~383 K (+110°C)
• Layer 2: ~253 K (−20°C)
• Layer 3: ~183 K (−90°C)
• Layer 4: ~133 K (−140°C)
• Layer 5 (cold): <50 K (−223°C)

The effective sun protection factor is ~1,000,000×. Solar flux of 1,360 W/m² is reduced to <1 mW/m² on the cold side. Five layers was the minimum needed to meet thermal requirements with adequate margin while remaining practical for deployment.

Tlayer n ≈ Tsun-side × (1/2)n/2
383 K → 36 K across 5 layers with vacuum gaps
Everyday parallel: It's exactly the principle behind a multi-pane window — but instead of reducing heat conduction through gas, it reduces radiative transfer through vacuum. Each pane absorbs and re-emits, losing energy at each step. A double-pane window drops ~40% of heat flow; JWST's 5-layer shield drops ~99.999% of solar heat flux. The sun delivers ~1,360 W/m² at L2; by the cold side, only ~23 milliwatts/m² leak through.

Silicon coating on Layer 1

The outermost layer faces the full fury of the Sun: UV, charged particles, and micrometeorites. The silicon coating on the sun side serves as a UV and charged particle shield — it reflects solar UV (which would degrade the Kapton underneath) and conducts away static charge from the solar wind. Without it, the Kapton would embrittle and flake within a few years.

Structural Composites

Carbon fibre, but not as you know it

The backplane and truss: CFRP composites

The structure holding the 18 mirror segments is a carbon fibre reinforced polymer (CFRP) composite backplane — specifically high-modulus pitch carbon fibres (K13C2U or M55J, axial modulus 540–590 GPa — about 2.5× standard aerospace fibre) in a cyanate ester resin matrix (RS-3 or EX-1515). This is not ordinary automotive or aerospace CFRP:

Near-zero CTE: The fibres have a slightly negative CTE (−1.5 × 10⁻⁶/K axially) while the resin has a positive CTE. By carefully controlling fibre orientation and volume fraction (60–65%), the composite achieves a net CTE of <0.5 × 10⁻⁶/K — essentially zero.

Cryogenic performance: Cyanate ester resins were chosen over standard epoxy because they absorb ~10× less moisture (critical since desorption in vacuum causes dimensional shifts), have higher strain-to-failure, and don't microcrack during cryogenic thermal cycling. Standard epoxies become brittle below −100°C and develop crack networks. Cyanate esters remain ductile to below −250°C.

Outgassing: Space-grade CFRP undergoes extensive bake-out (months at elevated temperature in vacuum) to drive out trapped volatiles, then is thermally "seasoned" (cycled between cryo and room temperature dozens of times) until dimensional behaviour stabilises. Only then is it precision-machined.

Length stability achieved: <0.5 μm/m per degree K near operating temperature — critical struts maintain mirror alignment to within 2 μm over the full operating temperature range.

Why not metal? An aluminium truss supporting the mirrors would expand/contract ~23 μm per metre per degree C. Over a 3-metre backplane, a 1°C temperature variation would produce ~69 μm of distortion — that's 460× the mirror alignment tolerance. The whole telescope would go out of focus with every tiny thermal fluctuation. The CFRP backplane barely notices temperature changes at all.

Invar and composite struts

The connections between the backplane and the spacecraft bus use Invar 36 (Fe–36%Ni) combined with composite bipod struts. These provide a thermally isolating "bridge" between the warm bus (~300 K) and the cold optical assembly (~40 K) while maintaining rigid alignment.

How Invar works: The CTE anomaly is caused by magnetostriction — the magnetic ordering of nickel atoms creates a lattice contraction that precisely compensates normal thermal expansion. CTE at room temperature: ~1.2 × 10⁻⁶/K (vs ~12 for steel). At 40 K: ~0.5 × 10⁻⁶/K. Total contraction from 293 K to 40 K: just 0.003% (~30 ppm).

CTE matching at joints: The composite struts (CTE ~0–0.5 × 10⁻⁶/K) closely match the Invar fittings (~0.5–1.2 × 10⁻⁶/K), minimising thermomechanical stress at adhesive bond lines during cooldown. In the most critical locations, Super Invar (Fe–32Ni–5Co) with CTE ~0.3 × 10⁻⁶/K is used.

The entire structural philosophy: Invar at interfaces, high-modulus pitch CFRP for struts, cyanate ester for moisture immunity — every element chosen for what it does best in its temperature range.

Detector Materials

Quantum engineering at the atomic level

HgCdTe (Mercury Cadmium Telluride) — "MerCadTel"

The near-IR detectors are built from Hg₁₋ₓCd_xTe — a ternary semiconductor whose bandgap can be continuously tuned by adjusting the Mercury-to-Cadmium ratio x:

• More Hg → smaller bandgap → sensitive to longer wavelengths (deeper IR)
• More Cd → larger bandgap → sensitive to shorter wavelengths (near-IR/visible)

For JWST's NIRCam (0.6–5 μm), x ≈ 0.3 gives a bandgap of ~0.25 eV — perfectly tuned to absorb photons in that range. The material is grown epitaxially (layer by atom layer) on CdZnTe substrates using molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) or liquid phase epitaxy (LPE).

Why it's remarkable: This single material system covers the entire 1–12 μm range just by changing composition. No other semiconductor family offers this tunability in the infrared.

Si:As (Arsenic-doped Silicon) — MIRI's detector

For the mid-IR (5–28 μm), photon energies are so low that even HgCdTe can't be made with a small enough bandgap reliably. Instead, MIRI uses extrinsic photoconductivity: arsenic atoms embedded in a silicon crystal create energy levels just 0.054 eV below the conduction band. Photons with wavelengths up to ~23 μm have enough energy to excite electrons from these impurity levels into the conduction band.

The "Blocked Impurity Band" (BIB) structure adds a thin undoped blocking layer that prevents dark current from impurity-band hopping conduction — dramatically reducing noise at the cost of requiring 7 K operating temperature.

Bandgap engineering in everyday terms: A bandgap is like the minimum height of a turnstile. Photons are people of different heights (energies). A large bandgap (tall turnstile) only lets energetic UV/visible "people" through. By tuning the HgCdTe ratio, you lower the turnstile so that less energetic infrared "people" can pass through too. Si:As goes further — it installs a step (the impurity level) so even very short mid-IR "people" can climb over.

Novel Material Applications

Actuator materials: Lead zirconate titanate (PZT) piezoelectrics

Each mirror segment has 7 actuators (6 for rigid-body positioning + 1 for radius of curvature) using piezoelectric materials that change length when voltage is applied. These achieve 10 nm positioning steps — at −233°C, after 20 years, thousands of adjustment cycles. The PZT ceramics must maintain their piezoelectric properties at cryogenic temperatures where many ferroelectric materials lose their response.

Microshutter material: LPCVD Silicon Nitride (Si₃N₄)

The 249,660 microshutter blades are made from sub-micrometre silicon nitride films deposited by Low-Pressure Chemical Vapour Deposition. Si₃N₄ was chosen because:

• Extreme strength-to-thickness ratio — can form sub-μm membranes that flex millions of times without fatigue
• Residual stress can be precisely controlled during deposition (critical for the "pre-curl" actuation mechanism)
• Remains elastic (non-brittle) at 35 K
• Optically opaque at relevant wavelengths — blocks unwanted light when closed

Thermal management materials

JWST manages a temperature gradient from 383 K to 7 K across a few metres. Key materials enabling this:

High-purity aluminium thermal straps (99.999% Al): Thermal conductivity exceeds 10,000 W/m·K at 40 K (phonon transport dominates). Used as heat links between instruments and radiators.

OFHC copper (C10100, 99.99% Cu): ~2,000 W/m·K at 40 K. Used at intermediate temperature stages.

Titanium alloy (Ti-6Al-4V) thermal isolators: Very low conductivity (<1 W/m·K at 40 K) bipod flexures serve as thermal breaks between warm and cold structures.

MLI blankets: Aluminized Mylar/Dacron mesh stacks. Carefully tailored — too much insulation prevents waste heat rejection; too little allows stray radiation ingress.

Black Kapton radiators: Carbon-loaded Kapton with emissivity >0.9, used on panels that reject heat to deep space.

The thermal conductivity surprise: High-purity aluminium at 40 K conducts heat 50× better than at room temperature. This is counterintuitive — but at cryogenic temperatures, phonon scattering decreases dramatically in pure metals. Impure metals (alloys) don't benefit from this. JWST exploits this by using five-nines-pure aluminium for thermal links and low-conductivity alloys for thermal breaks — same base element, radically different behaviour based on purity.

Unanswered Questions / Future Materials Challenges

  • Micrometeorite impacts are eroding the gold coating. How will 20 years of cumulative damage affect IR reflectivity?
  • Can silicon carbide (SiC) mirrors — lighter and stiffer than beryllium — be polished to the same nanometre precision for HWO?
  • Future coronagraph masks for direct exoplanet imaging need picometer-level stability. What materials can achieve this?
  • MIRI's detector performance is degrading slightly as cosmic ray damage accumulates in the Si:As lattice. Can this be annealed in-flight?

The materials limit on future telescopes

Here's something not often discussed: the next generation of space telescopes (HWO, the Habitable Worlds Observatory) needs a mirror 5-10× JWST's area to directly image Earth-like exoplanets. But the mirror stability requirement is 10 picometres — 15× tighter than JWST's already-insane 150 nm.

10 picometres is smaller than a single atom of silicon. No existing material can guarantee this level of dimensional stability over a multi-metre structure. The challenge isn't just finding a stiff, low-CTE material — it's understanding and controlling:

• Molecular relaxation: Metals and glasses slowly "creep" at the atomic level over years. We don't fully understand creep at picometre scales.
• Micrometeorite-induced stress: Each impact creates a tiny shock wave that permanently redistributes internal stress.
• Ageing effects: CTE itself can drift as crystal structure subtly evolves over decades.

Some candidates being investigated: ultra-low expansion glass ceramics (ULE, Zerodur) that were too heavy for JWST but might work with active deformable mirrors; silicon carbide (lighter than glass, stiffer than beryllium, but harder to polish); and even single-crystal silicon (perfectly uniform atomic structure, but can't be made large enough yet).

The stunning irony: our ability to build ever-larger telescopes may ultimately be limited not by funding, not by rocket size, not by optics design — but by our incomplete understanding of how materials behave at the picometre scale over decades. Materials science may be the final frontier of space telescope design.

HWO is in pre-Phase A study (as of 2026). The materials challenge is real and acknowledged in the community, but multiple approaches are being investigated. This is engineering-limited, not physics-limited — solutions likely exist but require significant R&D.
