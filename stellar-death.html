How stars die depends entirely on their mass

A star's fate is sealed at birth by one number: its initial mass. Everything else follows.

Low mass (< 8 M☉) — "Gentle" death: Stars like our Sun will exhaust their hydrogen fuel in ~10 billion years, swell into a red giant, shed their outer layers as a planetary nebula, and leave behind a white dwarf — a Earth-sized ember that slowly cools for trillions of years. No explosion. A slow fade.

High mass (> 8 M☉) — Violent death: Massive stars burn through their fuel in mere millions of years, fuse progressively heavier elements (He → C → O → Ne → Si → Fe), then collapse catastrophically when iron fills the core. Iron cannot release energy through fusion — the star's engine stalls, and the core implodes at 70,000 km/s. The result: a supernova, briefly outshining an entire galaxy, leaving behind a neutron star or black hole.

The fuel economy of mass: A star 10× the Sun's mass doesn't live 10× as long — it lives ~1,000× shorter. Why? Luminosity scales roughly as M³·⁵, so a 10 M☉ star burns fuel ~3,000× faster than the Sun. It's like comparing a candle (burns for hours) to a flare (blindingly bright, gone in minutes). The brightest stars are the most short-lived.

Planetary Nebulae: not planets at all

The term "planetary nebula" is a historical accident — through early telescopes they looked round and green, vaguely planet-like. They're actually the shed outer envelopes of dying low-mass stars, expanding into space at 20–30 km/s and illuminated by the ultraviolet radiation of the exposed stellar core (now a white dwarf at 100,000+ K).

JWST's infrared vision reveals something Hubble couldn't: the molecular chemistry in these shells. As the hot gas expands and cools, molecules form — H₂, CO, SiO, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs). These are the seeds of interstellar chemistry — the raw materials from which future solar systems (and ultimately, future life) will assemble.

What JWST revealed about the Southern Ring Nebula

The Day-1 image shocked researchers. Previous observations assumed a single dying star. JWST's infrared penetration revealed:

1. A binary system: Two stars at the centre — the hot white dwarf creating the nebula AND a cooler companion star. Their orbital interaction sculpted the complex ring structures.

2. At least 2–3 prior ejection episodes: Multiple concentric shells visible in infrared, each from a different mass-loss event thousands of years apart.

3. A possible third star: The shapes of some arcs suggest gravitational perturbation from a third, unseen companion — making this potentially a triple system.

4. Molecular richness: The outer shells glow with PAH emission (shown in blue/cyan in MIRI images) — complex organic molecules being dispersed into the interstellar medium.

Why this matters for us: Our solar system formed from a cloud of gas enriched by previous generations of stellar death. The calcium in your teeth was made in a supernova. The carbon in your DNA was fused in a red giant. The oxygen you're breathing was cooked in a massive star's core. Planetary nebulae and supernovae are the universe's recycling system — without stellar death, there would be no rocky planets and no life.

Supernovae and Their Remnants

Supernova Remnants: the aftermath of catastrophe

When a massive star explodes, it ejects its outer layers at 10,000–30,000 km/s — generating a blast wave that sweeps up interstellar gas, heats it to millions of degrees, and creates expanding shells of shocked material visible for tens of thousands of years.

JWST has observed supernova remnants like Cassiopeia A (Cas A) — the remnant of a star that exploded ~340 years ago (the light reached Earth around 1680). At a distance of ~11,000 light-years, it's one of the youngest known remnants in our galaxy.

Cassiopeia A through JWST's eyes

JWST's mid-infrared view of Cas A (released April 2023) revealed:

"Green Monster": A previously unknown large structure of warm dust in the interior of the remnant, whose origin remains debated — possibly a light echo or a distinct shock layer.

Unshocked ejecta: Knots of pristine supernova material that haven't yet encountered the reverse shock — original nucleosynthesis products frozen in time since the explosion.

Dust production: Young supernovae are major dust factories. Cas A appears to contain ~0.1 solar masses of dust — freshly manufactured from supernova ejecta. This is important because it may explain where the enormous quantities of dust in the early universe came from (a long-standing puzzle).

Supernova 1987A — the nearest supernova in 400 years

SN 1987A exploded in the Large Magellanic Cloud (168,000 light-years away) on February 23, 1987. It was visible to the naked eye and is the most-studied supernova in history. JWST observed it in 2023 and detected:

Newly formed molecules: CO and SiO in the inner ejecta — evidence of molecular chemistry occurring in supernova debris.

The central compact object: For 36 years, astronomers debated whether SN 1987A left behind a neutron star or a black hole. JWST's NIRSpec detected emission lines from ionised argon and sulfur that strongly suggest a young neutron star at the centre, hidden behind dense ejecta. This would be the youngest known neutron star.

Nucleosynthesis as an onion: A massive star's core just before it explodes looks like an onion — concentric shells of progressively heavier elements (H→He→C→O→Ne→Mg→Si→Fe from outside to inside). Each shell is the ash of the previous burning stage. When the star explodes, these layers are ejected and mixed into space. JWST's spectrographs can identify which elements are present in supernova remnants — effectively reading the recipe book of cosmic element creation.

White Dwarfs, Neutron Stars, and the Extremes

White dwarfs: quantum pressure vs gravity

When a low-mass star exhausts its fuel, the core (now pure carbon and oxygen) collapses until electron degeneracy pressure halts it — a quantum mechanical effect where electrons resist being packed closer together (Pauli exclusion principle). The result: a star the mass of the Sun compressed to the size of Earth. Density: ~10⁹ kg/m³ (a teaspoon weighs 5 tonnes).

The maximum mass a white dwarf can sustain is the Chandrasekhar limit: 1.4 M☉. Above this, electron pressure cannot resist gravity and the star collapses further.

Neutron stars: when atoms cease to exist

If the collapsing core exceeds 1.4 M☉, electrons are forced into protons (p + e⁻ → n + ν), converting the entire core into neutrons. Neutron degeneracy pressure halts the collapse at ~10 km radius. Density: ~10¹⁷ kg/m³ — nuclear density. A teaspoon weighs ~6 billion tonnes.

JWST contributes by studying the environments around neutron stars — the supernova remnants that created them, and the neutron star's effect on surrounding material. The SN 1987A detection of a probable young neutron star is a landmark finding.

Chandrasekhar limit: MCh ≈ 1.4 M☉ ≈ 2.8 × 10³⁰ kg
Compression comparison: If you compressed the Sun to a white dwarf, it would fit inside Earth. If you compressed it to a neutron star, it would fit inside a mid-sized city (~20 km diameter). If you compressed it further past the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit (~2.2 M☉), it would collapse into a black hole — an object with no stable configuration at any size. The event horizon of a 1 solar-mass black hole is just 6 km across.

Unanswered Questions

  • Is there a neutron star at the centre of SN 1987A? JWST's evidence is strong but not conclusive — it could be obscured emission from the ejecta itself.
  • How much dust can a single supernova produce? Early-universe dust budgets don't add up — we need more measurements like Cas A.
  • What shapes planetary nebulae? Many are bipolar, point-symmetric, or have jets. Binary companions? Magnetic fields? Planets?
  • Where are the Population III supernova remnants? The very first stars should have exploded billions of years ago — can JWST find evidence of their unique nucleosynthesis signatures in the oldest galaxies?

Stellar death as the universe's reproductive cycle

There's a beautiful feedback loop in stellar death that borders on the philosophical: stars die, seeding the interstellar medium with heavy elements and dust. That dust and gas collapses to form new stars with planets — something that was impossible for the first generation of stars (which formed from pure hydrogen/helium, with no rocky materials available).

Each generation of stellar death increases the "metallicity" (astronomer's term for anything heavier than helium) of the universe. The first stars: zero metallicity. Our Sun: ~1.3% metals by mass. Stars forming today in dense regions: up to 3×. This enrichment enables ever-more-complex chemistry — eventually producing the conditions for life.

In a very real sense, stellar death is not an ending — it's reproduction. Stars die so that more complex stars (and planets, and people) can be born. The universe is building complexity through sacrifice. JWST watches this cycle unfold across 13 billion years of cosmic history in a single observing programme.

This is interpretive framing, not speculative physics — the chemistry is established. But the narrative of stellar death as "generative" rather than "destructive" reflects how JWST is revealing connections between stellar evolution and the origin of habitable worlds.
