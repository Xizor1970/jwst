The Telescope That Ate Astronomy

A 2010 Nature article gave JWST this nickname — and it wasn't a compliment

The budget that wouldn't stop growing

JWST's cost history is a case study in mega-project escalation:

1997: ~$1 billion (original estimate, 2007 launch)
2002: $2.5 billion
2005: $4.5 billion (major re-plan)
2010: $6.5 billion (Casani review)
2011: $8.7 billion (post-cancellation rebaseline)
2021 (final): $9.7 billion ($8.8B development + $861M operations)

From $1B to $9.7B is a 10× overrun. From 2007 to 2021 is a 14-year delay. By 2010, JWST consumed one out of every three dollars in NASA's astrophysics budget. Other missions were cancelled or indefinitely delayed to feed it.

Perspective on the money: $10 billion over 25 years is ~$400 million/year — roughly the cost of two Hollywood blockbuster films per year, or about 0.007% of US federal spending. The US spends more on military air conditioning ($20B/year) than on JWST's entire development. But within NASA's science budget ($7B/year), JWST was a monster that starved other programmes.

The 2011 Near-Death Experience

July 6, 2011: Cancellation proposed

Representative Frank Wolf (R-VA), chairman of the House Appropriations Commerce/Justice/Science subcommittee, released a bill with zero funding for JWST — outright cancellation. The committee cited "billions of dollars over budget and plagued by poor management."

At that point, $3 billion had already been spent and 75% of hardware was in production. Cancelling would have meant writing off every dollar spent and losing the scientific capability entirely.

September 14, 2011: Saved by one senator

Senator Barbara Mikulski (D-MD) — whose state hosts the Space Telescope Science Institute and Goddard Space Flight Center — championed JWST in the Senate Appropriations Committee. Her subcommittee approved $530 million for JWST (40% more than even the White House requested). The vote: 15 to 1.

In November 2011, through House-Senate conference negotiations, Congress reversed the cancellation and capped total development at $8 billion with enhanced oversight.

What cancellation would have meant: No deep-field images of the first galaxies. No exoplanet atmospheric chemistry. No resolution of the Hubble Tension. No Southern Ring Nebula. No Pillars of Creation in infrared. Everything you've read in this entire knowledge base — gone. The margin between "greatest space telescope ever built" and "most expensive thing we threw away" was one Senate committee vote.

Why It Cost So Much

The Casani Report (November 2010)

The Independent Comprehensive Review Panel, chaired by JPL's John Casani, investigated the cost overruns. Key findings:

1. The design was technically sound. The money wasn't wasted on a bad design — it was wasted on underestimating a good one.

2. Initial cost estimates were "faulty." The original $1B estimate was never realistic for what was proposed. It was "not a reflection of management incompetence" but rather systemic optimism bias in NASA's cost-estimating culture.

3. Funding was "just insufficient to carry out the work." The annual budget allocation was too low for the schedule, creating delays that compounded costs (standing armies of engineers waiting for funding).

4. Chronic management problems included inadequate reserves, overly concurrent development (testing everything simultaneously rather than sequentially), and insufficient risk management.

The human errors of 2018

The final major delays (2018–2020) came from preventable mistakes during integration and testing:

• Engineers used the wrong solvent to clean propulsion valves, damaging them
• Dozens of sunshield screws came loose during vibration testing
• The sunshield fabric tore during practice deployment
• Cable tensioning systems didn't work as designed

These errors added ~$800 million and 2+ years to the schedule. They also led to the appointment of Gregory L. Robinson as programme director — who raised schedule efficiency from 50% to 95% within months and drove the project to completion. He was named TIME's Innovator of the Year in 2022.

The Human Story

The scale of the effort

10,000+ people worked on JWST across 258 organisations in 14 countries and 29 US states. Key contributors:

NASA (lead, ~$8.8B): Overall management (Goddard Space Flight Center), operations (STScI, Baltimore), NIRCam (University of Arizona).

Northrop Grumman: Prime contractor — spacecraft bus, sunshield, integration. Redondo Beach, California.

Ball Aerospace: 18 beryllium mirror segments, optical telescope element, wavefront sensing & control.

ESA (~€700M): NIRSpec instrument (Airbus, Germany), 50% of MIRI, Ariane 5 launch vehicle. In exchange: full partnership and guaranteed observing time for European astronomers.

CSA (~CA$200M): Fine Guidance Sensor (FGS) and NIRISS instrument.

Key figures

Dan Goldin — NASA Administrator (1992–2001). Rejected the "modest" 4-metre concept, pushed for 8 metres. "Such a modest thing" — his dismissal that set JWST on an ambitious (and expensive) path.

John C. Mather — Nobel laureate (Physics 2006, for COBE). JWST Senior Project Scientist since 1995. Provided scientific continuity across 25 years of development.

Gregory L. Robinson — Appointed 2018 after crisis. Turned the programme around. TIME 100 list. Retired July 2022 after first images.

Thomas Zurbuchen — NASA Associate Administrator. Called Robinson "the most effective leader of a mission I have ever seen in the history of NASA."

The Naming Controversy

Who was James Webb?

James E. Webb (1906–1992) served as NASA's 2nd administrator from 1961 to 1968 — leading the agency through Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo. He is widely credited with building NASA's institutional strength. Previously, he served as Under Secretary of State (1949–1952) under Truman.

The accusation

In March 2021, a group led by cosmologist Chanda Prescod-Weinstein urged NASA to rename the telescope, citing Webb's senior role at the State Department during the "Lavender Scare" — the systematic purge of LGBTQ+ employees from federal service in the 1950s. Critics also pointed to the firing of a NASA employee for a "homosexual advance" while Webb was administrator.

NASA's response

NASA commissioned an investigation examining over 50,000 documents. Their November 2022 report concluded: "No available evidence directly links Webb to any actions or follow-up related to the firing of individuals for their sexual orientation." Historian David K. Johnson (author of The Lavender Scare) stated he "knows of no evidence that Webb led or instigated persecution."

Decision: NASA did not change the name. Some astronomers continued to protest. The debate remains unresolved in parts of the community.

Christmas Day Launch

December 25, 2021, 12:20 UTC

After 25 years of development, 14 years of delays, a near-cancellation, a pandemic, human errors, and $9.7 billion — JWST launched on an Ariane 5 rocket from Kourou, French Guiana on Christmas morning.

The launch was so precise that it saved years of station-keeping fuel, directly extending the mission's operational lifetime. All 344 single-point-of-failure deployment mechanisms worked. Within six months, the first images were released — and the scientific returns have exceeded every prediction.

President Biden unveiled the first full-colour image at the White House on July 11, 2022: "This telescope embodies how America leads the world, not by the example of our power, but the power of our example."

The Hard Questions

  • Was JWST worth it? At $10B, it displaced other missions — was the scientific return worth the opportunity cost?
  • Could it have been done cheaper? The Casani report suggests yes, with better initial cost estimation and consistent funding. A slower start with realistic budgets would have avoided the political near-death.
  • Should one project ever consume 33% of a division's budget? How does NASA prevent this from recurring with HWO?
  • The naming controversy: should scientific instruments be named after administrators at all, or only after the science they enable?

What if it had been cancelled?

In an alternate 2011, JWST dies in committee. What happens?

The $3 billion already spent is written off. The 10,000 people disperse. The mirror segments, already polished to nanometre precision, go into storage (or a museum). The international partnerships with ESA and CSA collapse. Europe's €700M investment is lost. Trust in NASA's ability to execute flagship missions evaporates.

There would be no infrared successor to Hubble for at least 20–30 years (the time to design, fund, and build a replacement). The first galaxies, exoplanet atmospheres, and the Hubble Tension confirmation would all remain unknown. Ground-based telescopes can partially compensate, but not for mid-infrared or deep-field sensitivity.

Most likely: astronomy would have continued, but slower and with less certainty. The "impossible early galaxies" might not be discovered until the 2040s. The DMS hint on K2-18b — never detected. The Enceladus plume — measured from the ground at far lower precision.

JWST was not cancelled because one senator from Maryland decided it was worth fighting for. The margin was thin.

Counterfactuals are inherently unprovable. Other missions might have been funded in JWST's absence that produced different discoveries. But no planned or proposed telescope could replicate JWST's combined infrared sensitivity and spectroscopic capability — it was genuinely unique.
