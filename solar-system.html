Not Just a Deep-Space Telescope

JWST's infrared vision reveals chemistry and dynamics invisible from the ground

Why JWST is powerful for solar system science

JWST was designed to see the first galaxies. But its infrared capabilities make it extraordinarily useful for our own neighbourhood:

Molecular fingerprints: Water, CO₂, methane, and other key molecules have their strongest spectral features in the infrared (1–28 μm) — many of which are blocked by Earth's atmosphere for ground-based telescopes.

Moving target tracking: JWST can track objects moving up to 75 milliarcseconds/second — fast enough for asteroids, moons, and planets.

Sensitivity: It can detect features a million times dimmer than the parent planet (like Jupiter's rings) in a single exposure.

Constraint: JWST cannot observe Mercury, Venus, Earth, or the Moon — objects must be more than 85° from the Sun to avoid illuminating the cold side.

Jupiter: Auroras and Hidden Jet Streams

Aurora imaging (July 2022)

JWST captured unprecedented views of Jupiter's polar auroras — bright enough to create diffraction spikes in the optics. Infrared emission from ionised hydrogen (H₃⁺) extends to high altitudes at both poles.

A puzzling discovery within Io's auroral footprint: a cold spot at 538 K surrounded by 766 K aurora, but with 3× the density of the main aurora — "the highest densities we have ever recorded." It appeared in only one of five snapshots, meaning it fluctuates on timescales of minutes.

A hidden jet stream (October 2023)

By comparing NIRCam images taken 10 hours apart (one Jupiter rotation) at 2.12 μm, JWST discovered a previously invisible equatorial jet stream:

Speed: 515 km/h — twice the sustained winds of a Category 5 hurricane
Width: >4,800 km
Altitude: ~40 km above the cloud tops, in the lower stratosphere
Significance: Invisible at other wavelengths; only detectable in this narrow infrared window. It demonstrates that Jupiter's atmosphere has layered wind systems we've never seen.

Jupiter's rings: JWST captured Jupiter's faint ring system — a million times dimmer than the planet — in the same image as background galaxies billions of light-years away. Tiny moon Adrastea (~20 km across) is visible at the ring edge. It's like photographing a candle flame AND the Eiffel Tower in a single shot, perfectly exposed for both.

Saturn: Rings That Outshine the Planet

Methane-dark Saturn (July 2023)

At 3.23 μm, methane absorbs almost all sunlight hitting Saturn's atmosphere — making the planet appear nearly black. But the methane-free ice rings reflect brightly, creating a surreal image where the rings outshine the planet they orbit.

Rings resolved: the dark C ring, bright B ring, narrow Cassini Division, A ring with the Encke Gap, and the thin F ring. Three moons visible: Dione, Enceladus, and Tethys.

Saturn's spin mystery solved (May 2026)

JWST observed Saturn's northern aurora continuously for an entire Saturnian day using NIRSpec. By mapping H₃⁺ emission, researchers discovered that aurora drives a self-sustaining energy cycle: auroral energy heats the atmosphere, generating winds that create electrical currents, which then power the aurora again. JWST measurements were ~10× more precise than any previous observation of Saturn's aurora.

Ocean Worlds: Europa and Enceladus

Europa: Carbon from the ocean (September 2023)

Two papers published simultaneously in Science confirmed CO₂ on Europa's surface — concentrated in Tara Regio, a geologically young "chaos terrain" where surface ice has been disrupted from below.

Why this matters: The carbon almost certainly originated from Europa's subsurface ocean, not from external meteorite delivery. It was deposited recently (CO₂ is unstable on Europa's radiation-bombarded surface). This demonstrates the ocean contains carbon — a fundamental requirement for life as we know it.

NIRSpec mapped the CO₂ distribution at 320 × 320 km per pixel. Hubble had previously found ocean-derived salt in the same region — building a picture of a chemically complex, potentially habitable ocean.

Enceladus: A water plume 20× the moon's size (May 2023)

JWST detected a water vapour plume from Enceladus extending more than 9,600 km — over 20× the diameter of the moon itself (504 km). Water ejection rate: approximately 300 litres per second through "tiger stripe" fractures in the icy crust.

The water torus: Enceladus orbits Saturn every 33 hours, leaving behind a donut-shaped halo of water vapour co-located with Saturn's E-ring. About 30% stays in the torus; 70% escapes to supply the broader Saturnian system.

For comparison: Cassini mapped jets extending only hundreds of kilometres. JWST revealed the plume at 10–50× greater scale than ever previously seen.

Enceladus in perspective: A 504 km moon (smaller than the distance from London to Edinburgh) producing a water plume that extends 9,600 km — the equivalent of spraying water from your garden hose and having the stream reach from Cape Town to Tokyo. Driven by tidal heating from Saturn's gravity flexing the moon's core. There is a global saltwater ocean beneath the ice, with hydrothermal vents on the ocean floor. All the ingredients for life.

Neptune's rings — first clear view in 33 years (September 2022)

The clearest image of Neptune's ring system since Voyager 2 in 1989. JWST resolved all ring components: Galle (innermost), LeVerrier, Lassell, Arago, and the bright Adams ring with its clumpy arcs. Seven moons detected, including Triton (reflecting 70% of incoming light — likely a captured Kuiper Belt object).

At 3 μm, methane absorption makes Neptune appear dark (like Saturn), while the rings and atmospheric features pop into view.

Neptune's cooling atmosphere (March 2025)

JWST detected H₃⁺ in Neptune's atmosphere for the first time and found the upper atmosphere has cooled by several hundred degrees since Voyager 2's flyby in 1989. Neptune's auroras appear at mid-latitudes (not poles) because its magnetic field is tilted 47° from the rotation axis — unlike any other planet.

Uranus: rings and polar vortex (2023)

JWST imaged 11 of Uranus's 13 known rings (including the diffuse Zeta ring, previously only seen by Voyager 2). A bright polar cap with a cyclonic vortex at its centre was resolved for the first time, along with storm clouds at the cap's edge. A December 2023 follow-up detected 14 of 27 moons in a single frame.

Titan: Watching Chemistry Happen

The methyl radical — a missing piece (2025)

JWST made the first detection of methyl radical (CH₃) in Titan's atmosphere — a molecule that forms when sunlight splits methane, then recombines to create ethane and heavier hydrocarbons that rain onto the surface forming lakes and seas.

As NASA's Stefanie Milam put it: "For the first time we can see the chemical cake while it's rising in the oven, instead of just the starting ingredients."

JWST also detected clouds in Titan's northern hemisphere — the first evidence of convective cloud activity during northern summer (previously only seen in the south by Cassini).

Asteroids and the Outer Reaches

DART impact observation (September 2022)

JWST observed NASA's DART spacecraft slam into asteroid Dimorphos in real-time — capturing ejecta streaming away over 5 hours in 10 sequential images. The asteroid was moving 3× faster than JWST's original tracking speed limit, requiring special operational accommodations.

Tiny main-belt asteroids (2024)

Using MIRI at 15 μm with GPU-based synthetic tracking, JWST detected 139 previously unknown asteroids — some as small as ~10 metres across. These are among the smallest main-belt objects ever observed, revealing an active collisional cascade (larger bodies grinding into smaller ones). The observations were serendipitous — captured in background data from TRAPPIST-1 exoplanet observations.

Kuiper Belt Objects

The Disco-TNOs programme observed ~60 trans-Neptunian objects (including Sedna, Gonggong, Quaoar) starting late 2022 — providing the first detailed compositional study of these primordial remnants. These objects are time capsules from the solar system's formation, and may explain how Earth acquired its water.

Unanswered Questions

  • Is Europa's ocean habitable? JWST found carbon there, but what about energy sources and electron acceptors? Europa Clipper (launched 2024) will follow up.
  • Does Enceladus have sustained geothermal energy at its ocean floor? The plume composition hints at hydrothermal activity, but is it enough for life?
  • Why has Neptune's atmosphere cooled so dramatically since 1989? Seasonal effects on a 165-year orbit, or something unexpected?
  • What is Titan's full hydrocarbon chemistry? JWST found CH₃ — but the pathways from methane to the complex tholins (organic hazes) coating Titan remain partially mapped.

Are we looking for life in the wrong place?

The classic "habitable zone" — the Goldilocks band where liquid water can exist on a planet's surface — applies to stars. But Enceladus and Europa are both far outside the Sun's habitable zone. Their oceans are liquid because of tidal heating from their giant-planet hosts, not stellar radiation.

This suggests a radical expansion of where life might exist: any icy moon around any gas giant, at any distance from its star, if tidal forces are strong enough. The Milky Way has far more gas giants (and therefore moons) than it has Sun-like stars with rocky planets in habitable zones. If tidal heating can sustain subsurface oceans — and JWST is confirming that it does — then the habitable real estate of the galaxy is vastly larger than we thought.

Enceladus sprays its ocean contents into space. You don't even need to drill through the ice — you just need to fly through the plume and taste it. A future mission could sample Enceladus's ocean without ever landing.

No life has been detected anywhere beyond Earth. The presence of water, carbon, and energy is necessary but not sufficient. The gap between "habitable" and "inhabited" remains the biggest open question in astrobiology.
