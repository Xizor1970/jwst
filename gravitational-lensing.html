Einstein's Telescope

Massive objects bend spacetime — and with it, the path of light

The physics

General Relativity predicts that mass curves spacetime. Light follows curved spacetime. Therefore, light from a distant source bends as it passes a massive foreground object — just as glass bends light in an optical lens. The effect creates distorted, magnified, and sometimes multiple images of the background source.

Three regimes:

Strong lensing: Dramatic distortion — arcs, multiple images, Einstein rings. Occurs when source, lens, and observer are closely aligned and the lens is massive (galaxy clusters, >10¹⁴ M☉).

Weak lensing: Subtle statistical distortions in background galaxy shapes, detectable only by averaging thousands of galaxies. Maps the total mass distribution (including dark matter).

Microlensing: Individual stars in the lens create brief, extreme magnification events when a background object crosses a "caustic" — a line of theoretically infinite magnification.

The wine glass analogy: Set a wine glass on a printed page and look through the base. You'll see the text distorted into arcs, stretched, magnified, sometimes duplicated. A galaxy cluster does the same thing to the light from galaxies behind it — except the "glass" is curved spacetime, and the distortion encodes the mass distribution of the lens. Reading the distortion tells you where the mass is — including dark matter that emits no light.

Why lensing is so powerful for JWST

Gravitational lensing effectively turns galaxy clusters into cosmic telescopes. A magnification factor μ means a source appears μ times brighter — equivalent to increasing JWST's mirror diameter by √μ.

At the highest magnifications (~10,000×), the combined JWST + gravitational lens system is equivalent to a 600-metre diameter telescope. Nothing we can build achieves this. Nature provides free magnification.

Effective diameter = DJWST × √μ = 6.5 m × √10,000 = 650 m equivalent

Earendel — The Most Distant Star

A single star from 900 million years after the Big Bang

Name: WHL0137-LS ("Earendel" — Old English for "morning star")
Redshift: z = 6.2
Light travel time: 12.9 billion years
Discovery: March 2022 (Hubble); confirmed July 2022 (JWST)
Magnification: Originally estimated 1,000–40,000×; revised to 43–67× (2025)

Earendel sits precisely on the critical curve of cluster WHL0137-08 — a line where magnification peaks sharply. On either side, features in the host galaxy (the "Sunrise Arc") are mirrored, but Earendel appears as a single unresolved point — consistent with an individual star (or tight binary).

Physical properties (if single star): Spectral type B, temperature 13,000–16,000 K, mass 20–200 M☉, luminosity ~1 million L☉. A possible cooler companion was detected in JWST colour data.

2025 debate: New analysis suggests Earendel might be a compact star cluster rather than a single star. The question remains open — and either interpretation is extraordinary at z = 6.2.

Finding Earendel: Detecting a single star at z = 6.2 without lensing would require a telescope ~100× JWST's collecting area — something that won't exist for centuries. Gravitational lensing gives us this for free, but only along the critical curve — a razor-thin line in the sky. Earendel happened to land exactly on that line. A few arcseconds to either side, and it would be invisible.

The Sunrise Arc and Lensed Star Zoo

The Sunrise Arc — most magnified galaxy at z > 6

Earendel's host galaxy stretches >15 arcseconds across the sky as a crescent — the longest gravitational arc of any galaxy in the universe's first billion years. JWST resolved individual star clusters (as small as 10 light-years across) and identified regions younger than 5 million years alongside clusters older than 10 million years — watching a galaxy's stellar population assemble in real time.

Other extraordinarily magnified stars

Mothra (z = 2.091): A binary supergiant system magnified ~4,000–5,000×, visible continuously for >8 years (2014–2022+). This persistent magnification requires an additional "milli-lens" — a mass of 10⁴–10⁶ M☉ along the line of sight (a dark matter subhalo?). Mothra constrains warm dark matter particles below 8.7 keV.

Godzilla (z = 2.37): Possibly the most luminous lensed star candidate, magnified 600–25,000× (model-dependent). Luminosity: 134–255 million L☉. An Eta Carinae analogue — a massive, unstable star about to explode.

The "Christmas Tree Cluster" (MACS J0416): JWST monitoring over 4 months found 14 transient sources — 12 are microlensed stars winking in and out as they cross micro-caustics. The cluster twinkles like a Christmas tree.

SN H0pe — A Supernova That Measures the Universe

A triply-imaged Type Ia supernova

Redshift: z = 1.783 (3.5 billion years after the Big Bang)
Classification: Type Ia — a "standard candle" with known intrinsic brightness
Lensing cluster: PLCK G165.7+67.0 (z = 0.35)

Three images of the same supernova appear at different positions — each arriving at a different time because the light paths have different lengths. The measured time delays:

• Image 2a arrives 116.6 days before image 2b
• Image 2c arrives 48.6 days before image 2b

Hubble constant measurement: H₀ = 75.4 (+8.1/−5.5) km/s/Mpc — consistent with local measurements and in tension with early-universe values. An entirely independent confirmation of the Hubble Tension via a method that doesn't rely on Cepheids or the CMB.

How time delays measure H₀: Imagine you and a friend both shout at the same time in a canyon. If you know the geometry (where the walls are), you can calculate the speed of sound from the difference in echo timing. Similarly: if you know the lens geometry (mass model), the time delay between lensed supernova images gives you the distance scale — and therefore H₀. No distance ladder needed. Pure geometry.

Dark Matter Mapping

Seeing the invisible

Gravitational lensing is the only way to map mass regardless of whether it emits light. Since ~85% of mass is dark matter, lensing reveals the universe's true mass distribution.

COSMOS-Web (January 2026): JWST's weak lensing survey measured shapes of ~250,000 galaxies across 0.54 deg² — producing the sharpest dark matter mass map ever made over this area (resolution: 1 arcmin, 2× better than Hubble). Prominent peaks correspond to known clusters. Faint filamentary structures connecting overdense regions were detected — "the strands of the cosmic web itself."

Substructure from Mothra: The persistent milli-lens magnifying Mothra has a mass of 10⁴–2.5×10⁶ M☉ — the smallest dark matter substructure ever constrained by lensing at z > 0.3. Its existence constrains the particle mass of warm dark matter and fuzzy/axion dark matter.

Unanswered Questions

  • Is Earendel a single star or a compact cluster? Future JWST spectroscopy may resolve this.
  • Can time-delay cosmography from lensed supernovae achieve the precision needed to definitively resolve the Hubble Tension?
  • How much dark matter substructure exists below 10⁶ M☉? This constrains whether dark matter is cold, warm, or fuzzy.
  • How many more caustic-crossing stars will JWST find? With enough examples, we could study individual stellar physics at cosmological distances.

Could lensing reveal the nature of dark matter?

Different dark matter candidates predict different amounts of substructure in lensing clusters:

Cold dark matter (CDM): Predicts abundant small subhalos down to Earth-mass scales (~10⁻⁶ M☉). These should perturb lensed images in detectable ways.

Warm dark matter (WDM): Subhalos below ~10⁸ M☉ are suppressed. Smoother mass distributions, fewer perturbations.

Fuzzy dark matter (FDM): Wave-like behaviour creates characteristic interference patterns and a hard cutoff in subhalo mass (~10⁷ M☉).

JWST's detection of the Mothra milli-lens already rules out some WDM and FDM parameter space. A systematic survey of caustic-crossing stars (each probing a random line of sight through a cluster) could statistically detect or exclude dark matter substructure at unprecedented scales. The "twinkling" of the Christmas Tree Cluster is, in a sense, dark matter substructure expressing itself as fluctuations in magnification.

We might solve the dark matter problem not by detecting a particle in a laboratory, but by watching stars blink through cosmic lenses.

Current constraints remain weak — they rule out extreme WDM/FDM models but cannot yet distinguish between CDM and moderate alternatives. Systematic surveys with JWST and Roman are planned but results are years away.
