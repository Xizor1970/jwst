Cosmic Cliffs — Carina Nebula (NGC 3324)

A towering wall of gas and dust ~7,600 light-years away, sculpted by intense ultraviolet radiation from young, massive stars above the frame. The "cliffs" are about 7 light-years tall — if you could drive along them at motorway speed (120 km/h), it would take about 63 million years. Dozens of previously hidden jets from newborn stars (Herbig-Haro objects) are visible for the first time — infrared light cuts through the dust that made this region opaque to Hubble.

Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

Why infrared reveals hidden births

Stars are born inside dense clouds of gas and dust. Visible light can't escape — it's absorbed by dust grains. But infrared light (longer wavelength) passes through dust the way radio waves pass through walls. JWST sees straight into these cocoons, revealing protostars, jets, and accretion disks that were completely invisible before.

Think of it like thermal imaging: a firefighter's IR camera sees through smoke to find people. JWST's IR detectors see through cosmic "smoke" to find baby stars.

Why dust blocks visible but not infrared: It's about wavelength vs particle size. Dust grains are typically 0.1–1 micrometre across. Visible light (0.4–0.7 μm) is the same scale as the grains — it scatters and absorbs. Infrared (1–28 μm) has wavelengths much larger than the grains — it slides past them like ocean waves passing through the pylons of a pier. Same reason your FM radio works through walls but your Wi-Fi struggles.

Pillars of Creation — Eagle Nebula (M16)

Perhaps the most iconic astronomical image ever taken, now reimagined by JWST. The pillars are columns of cold molecular hydrogen and dust, ~4–5 light-years tall. Bright red dots at the tips are newborn stars, only a few hundred thousand years old. The semi-transparency reveals that these pillars are thinner than they appeared in Hubble's image — we're watching them being eroded in real time by radiation from nearby massive stars.

Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

The Pillars' lifespan: These structures will be completely destroyed in ~3 million years by radiation erosion. That sounds long, but it's 0.02% of the universe's age. In human terms, if the universe were a 75-year-old person, these pillars have about 6 hours left to live. We're catching them in their final moments — cosmically speaking, we arrived just in time.

What we're learning about star formation

Feedback loops: Massive stars both destroy and trigger new star formation. Their radiation erodes gas pillars (destroying potential stars) while simultaneously compressing gas at pillar tips (triggering collapse into new stars). It's creation and destruction in a single frame.

Protoplanetary disks: JWST has imaged disks of gas and dust around newborn stars — the raw material for future planetary systems. We're watching solar systems being assembled.

Binary and multiple systems: Most stars form in pairs or groups, not alone. JWST's resolution is revealing how these systems emerge from collapsing cloud cores.

The stellar lifecycle in one image

The Carina Nebula image contains every stage of stellar life simultaneously:

1. Raw material: The dark, cold gas in the "cliff" — molecular hydrogen at ~10 K, waiting to collapse.

2. Collapse: Dense knots (Bok globules) where gravity is winning — future stars.

3. Protostars: Hidden inside the dust, detectable only in infrared — glowing from gravitational energy as they compress.

4. Young stars: The bright points along the cliff edge — they've ignited hydrogen fusion and are now blasting radiation into their birth cloud.

5. Destruction: The ionization front eating into the cliff from above — older massive stars obliterating the nursery that made them.

Unanswered Questions

  • What determines whether a collapsing gas core becomes one star or splits into a binary/multiple system?
  • How do planets begin forming while the star itself is still accreting material?
  • These pillars will be destroyed in ~3 million years. What fraction of the embedded protostars will survive to ignite?
  • Why do some regions form mostly massive stars while others produce swarms of small ones?
