The Next Generation

JWST opened doors that only bigger, more precise telescopes can walk through

Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope (launch: ~2027)

What it is: A 2.4-metre mirror (same size as Hubble) but with a field of view 100× wider. Think of it as Hubble with a panoramic lens. Also carries a coronagraph for directly imaging exoplanets.

What it'll do: Survey billions of galaxies to map dark energy's effect on cosmic expansion. Detect thousands of exoplanets via microlensing. Create the widest, deepest near-IR survey ever.

Relationship to JWST: Complementary — Roman finds the targets (wide surveys), JWST studies them in depth (narrow, deep). Together they'll map the universe's expansion history with unprecedented precision, potentially resolving the Hubble Tension.

Habitable Worlds Observatory (HWO, launch: ~2040s)

What it is: NASA's next flagship after JWST. A ~6-metre UV/optical/near-IR telescope with a coronagraph capable of directly imaging Earth-like planets around Sun-like stars.

The goal: Take actual photographs of rocky planets in habitable zones — resolve them as distinct points of light separate from their star — and analyse their atmospheres for biosignatures (O₂, O₃, H₂O, CH₄ together). JWST can characterise atmospheres of transiting planets, but transit geometry is rare. HWO will directly image planets regardless of orbital alignment.

The engineering challenge: Starlight is 10 billion times brighter than an Earth-like planet at visible wavelengths. The coronagraph must suppress this by a factor of 10¹⁰ while maintaining the planet's faint signal. This requires mirror stability of 10 picometres — smaller than an atom (see Materials section).

The contrast challenge: Imaging an Earth-like exoplanet next to its star is like spotting a firefly hovering 1 centimetre from a lighthouse — from 5,000 km away. And you need to determine the colour of the firefly. This is what HWO aims to do.

Ground-Based Giants

Extremely Large Telescope (ELT, first light: ~2028)

What it is: ESO's 39-metre segmented mirror telescope under construction in Chile's Atacama Desert. Five times Keck's diameter. Collecting area: 978 m² — about 38× JWST.

What it'll do: Resolve individual stars in galaxies out to 100 million light-years. Directly image gas giant exoplanets. Potentially detect the cosmic deceleration/acceleration transition directly. Measure the expansion of the universe in real time (the "redshift drift" — watching z change over a decade).

Limitations vs JWST: Atmosphere. Even with adaptive optics (laser guide stars, deformable mirrors correcting 1,000× per second), ground-based telescopes can't match JWST's infrared sensitivity or thermal background. Space will always win for faint IR science.

Giant Magellan Telescope (GMT, first light: ~2029)

Seven 8.4-metre mirrors arranged like a flower — combined aperture of 25.4 metres. Being built at Las Campanas Observatory, Chile. US-led counterpart to ELT.

Thirty Meter Telescope (TMT, first light: uncertain)

A 30-metre segmented mirror planned for Mauna Kea, Hawaii. Construction has been delayed by protests from Native Hawaiian communities who consider the summit sacred. Site selection controversy continues.

Resolution comparison: Angular resolution scales as wavelength/diameter. JWST (6.5 m, IR) achieves ~0.07 arcsec at 2 μm. ELT (39 m, visible) will achieve ~0.005 arcsec at 0.5 μm — about 14× sharper than JWST. But JWST's infrared sensitivity in space remains unmatched by anything on the ground. They serve different science cases.

What Questions Remain?

The questions JWST opened that only future facilities can answer

1. Are we alone? JWST can hint (K2-18b's DMS). HWO could confirm — by detecting oxygen, ozone, water, and methane together in a rocky planet's atmosphere around a Sun-like star. This combination has no known abiotic explanation.

2. What is dark energy? JWST + Roman will measure the expansion history. If dark energy varies with time (rather than being constant), it points to entirely new physics. ELT might detect "redshift drift" — watching the expansion change in real time.

3. What happened in the first 200 million years? JWST sees to z ~ 14–16. But the universe was dark before z ~ 30 (the "Cosmic Dawn"). Future far-IR and radio telescopes (or a JWST successor) may probe this truly uncharted era.

4. How did the first black holes form? JWST found impossibly massive early black holes. Direct collapse? Primordial? Merged from smaller seeds? Gravitational wave observations (LISA, launching ~2035) + JWST/HWO electromagnetic observations could trace the full formation history.

5. Is our cosmological model fundamentally wrong? If the Hubble Tension persists through Roman's precision measurements, ΛCDM may need replacement. What replaces it is anyone's guess.

The Roadmap

  • ~2027: Roman Space Telescope (wide-field surveys, dark energy, exoplanets)
  • ~2028: ELT first light (ground-based, 39 m, direct exoplanet imaging)
  • ~2029: GMT first light (ground-based, 25 m)
  • ~2035: LISA (space gravitational waves — merging black holes across the universe)
  • ~2040s: HWO (direct imaging of habitable exoplanets, biosignatures)
  • ~2045+: JWST fuel exhaustion (unless refuelled)

The 50-year view: what will we know by 2075?

If all goes according to plan — Roman, ELT, GMT, LISA, HWO — by 2075 we will likely:

• Know whether Earth-like planets with oxygen-rich atmospheres exist (dozens surveyed)
• Have resolved the Hubble Tension one way or another (new physics or refined measurements)
• Understand how the first black holes formed (LISA + electromagnetic observations)
• Have mapped the cosmic web in 3D out to z > 5
• Have directly measured the expansion of the universe changing in real time

And we will probably have discovered something nobody alive today has imagined. Every major telescope has found its most important discovery to be something it wasn't designed to detect. JWST's "impossible early galaxies" are exactly this — not in any pre-launch science case. HWO will find its own surprises.

The history of astronomy is the history of underestimating what's out there.

Timelines for space missions are notoriously optimistic. JWST itself was 14 years late. Treat all dates beyond Roman (~2027) as aspirational.
