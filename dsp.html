The Data Challenge

A 2003-era computer, a 25 Mbit/s straw, and the faintest signals in the universe

The on-board computer

JWST runs on a BAE Systems RAD750 processor — a radiation-hardened PowerPC 750 (Apple G3 era) clocked at 118 MHz with 44 MB of RAM and a 68 GB solid-state recorder. For context, this is roughly equivalent to a mid-1990s desktop computer. Your phone is about 1,000× more powerful.

Why so "slow"? Radiation. At L2, the processor is bombarded by cosmic rays and solar particles. Consumer chips would fail within days. The RAD750 is hardened to withstand 200 krad total ionizing dose and tolerates single-event upsets (bit flips from particle strikes) without crashing. It runs VxWorks, a real-time operating system. Reliability over raw speed — for a 20-year mission with no repair option, this is the only sane choice.

JWST actually uses a dual-computer architecture: one RAD750 for the spacecraft bus (attitude control, power, comms) and a dedicated ISIM Command and Data Handling unit for the science instruments, connected by SpaceWire point-to-point links.

Engineering tradeoff: It's like choosing between a Formula 1 car and a Land Rover Defender for a 20-year expedition across unmapped terrain with no mechanics for 1.5 million km. The F1 car is faster. The Defender will actually finish.

Data rates and the communication bottleneck

Raw data generated: Up to ~58.8 GB per day from all four instruments combined (vs Hubble's 1–2 GB/day). The instruments can produce up to 48 Mbps internally — about six 2048×2048 full-frame images every 10.7 seconds.

Downlink capacity: Ka-band at 25.9 GHz, maximum 28 Mbit/s (selectable: 7, 14, or 28 Mbps depending on weather at ground stations). Daily contact window: 4–6 hours via the Deep Space Network (Goldstone, Canberra, Madrid). Total daily downlink: ~270 Gbits.

The bottleneck: JWST generates more data than it can send. The 68 GB solid-state recorder holds roughly 24 hours of data before filling. Observations must be carefully scheduled to avoid overflowing it. JWST shares DSN time with ~40 other missions (Parker Solar Probe, Voyager, Mars orbiters).

Distance penalty: The signal from L2 takes ~5 seconds to reach Earth. The received power at the DSN 34-metre dishes is about 10⁻¹⁹ watts — roughly the energy of a single photon arriving every few nanoseconds. QPSK modulation with Reed-Solomon error correction reconstructs the data perfectly from this whisper.

An interesting design constraint: X-band communication was rejected partly because the larger antenna required would disturb the spacecraft's pointing stability during imaging. Ka-band's higher frequency allowed a smaller 0.6 m dish with equivalent gain.

Bandwidth perspective: 57 GB/day through a 26 Mbit/s link is like trying to empty a bathtub through a drinking straw — but you only get the straw for 4 hours a day. Everything must be prioritised, compressed, and scheduled. If JWST takes too many observations between contact windows, data is lost. The flight planners balance science ambition against downlink reality daily.
Daily data budget: 57 GB generated → ~29 GB compressed → 28.6 Gbit downlink capacity

The Detectors

Converting individual photons into numbers

Near-infrared detectors (NIRCam, NIRSpec, NIRISS): HgCdTe

The near-infrared instruments use Mercury Cadmium Telluride (HgCdTe) detector arrays — specifically Teledyne H2RG (HAWAII-2RG) sensors: 2048 × 2048 pixels, 18 μm pixel pitch, read by 4 amplifiers simultaneously. JWST has 17 of these arrays total (10 in NIRCam, 2 in NIRSpec, 1 in NIRISS, 2 in the Fine Guidance Sensor).

How they work: Incoming IR photons excite electrons across the HgCdTe bandgap. By adjusting the Mercury-to-Cadmium ratio (x ≈ 0.3 for long-wave, x ≈ 0.45 for short-wave), the bandgap is precisely tuned from 0.24 to 0.5 eV — selecting the cutoff wavelength. The HgCdTe layer is grown by molecular beam epitaxy on a CdZnTe substrate and indium-bump bonded to a silicon readout chip.

Read noise (CDS): 11–18 electrons single-read; reduced to <8 electrons with Fowler-8 sampling. NIRSpec's IRS2 mode achieves 6 electrons total.

Dark current: 0.002–0.004 e⁻/s/pixel at 37 K — extraordinarily low.

Quantum efficiency: 65–80% for short-wave, >95% (mean 96.8%) for long-wave. At short wavelengths, quantum yield actually exceeds unity — a single energetic photon generates multiple electron-hole pairs.

Operating temperature: 37–40 K (−236°C). Warmer than this, thermal dark current overwhelms the astronomical signal.

Operability: >99% of pixels functional — extraordinary uniformity.

Mid-infrared detector (MIRI): Si:As

MIRI uses Arsenic-doped Silicon (Si:As) Blocked Impurity Band (BIB) detectors — three arrays of 1024 × 1024 pixels, 25 μm pitch, manufactured by Raytheon. These respond to wavelengths from 5 to 28.5 μm.

How it works: Arsenic atoms in the silicon lattice create impurity energy levels just 54 meV below the conduction band. Mid-IR photons (too weak to cross silicon's full 1.1 eV bandgap) have enough energy to excite electrons from these impurity levels. A thin undoped "blocking" layer prevents thermally-driven hopping conduction that would otherwise produce enormous dark current.

Operating temperature: 6.2–7 K (−267°C). Dark current doubles for every ~2 K increase. Above 7.5 K, thermal noise overwhelms the signal. The pulse-tube/Joule-Thomson cryocooler delivers 55 mW of cooling power at 6.2 K — consuming 295 W of electrical power on the warm side.

Performance: Dark current 0.03–0.17 e⁻/s; read noise ~14 e⁻; well depth ~250,000 e⁻; QE 60–70%; operability >99.9%.

Detector noise floor: JWST's NIRCam detectors can detect signals as faint as ~1 photon per second per pixel, sustained over hours. That's like hearing a single whispered syllable per second in a quiet library — and determining exactly what word was spoken — after listening for 10 hours straight.

Signal Processing Pipeline

From raw photon counts to the images that rewrite textbooks

Step 1: Up-the-ramp sampling (on-board)

Unlike a camera that takes a single exposure, JWST detectors are read non-destructively multiple times during a single integration. Charge accumulates on each pixel (like water filling a bucket), and the detector is read at regular intervals without resetting — creating a "ramp" of increasing signal.

Why this matters:

• Cosmic ray rejection: A cosmic ray hitting a pixel creates a sudden jump in the ramp. By fitting a straight line to the ramp samples, the jump can be identified and removed. Without this, cosmic rays (which hit every ~10 seconds per detector) would ruin long exposures.

• Reduced read noise: Fitting a slope through N samples reduces effective read noise by ~√N. With 20+ reads, the 15-electron single-read noise drops to ~3–6 electrons effective.

• Dynamic range: Bright pixels that saturate early can still be measured from the initial (unsaturated) part of the ramp.

Engineering analogy: Up-the-ramp sampling is like measuring water flow by photographing a graduated cylinder every 10 seconds rather than reading it once at the end. If someone bumps the table at t = 40 s (cosmic ray), you can see the discontinuity in your time-series and correct for it. You also get a more precise flow rate by fitting a line through many measurements than from a single noisy reading.
Effective read noise ≈ single-read noise / √(Nreads) ≈ 15e⁻ / √20 ≈ 3.4e⁻

Step 2: Reference pixel subtraction and 1/f noise

Each H2RG detector has 4 rows and 4 columns of reference pixels around its edge — pixels that are not sensitive to light but experience the same electronic drifts as the active pixels. By subtracting the reference pixel signal, low-frequency electronic noise (1/f noise from the SIDECAR ASIC readout electronics) is removed.

This is directly analogous to differential signalling in PCB design — common-mode rejection of noise that affects both signal and reference equally.

The IRS2 breakthrough (NIRSpec only): Border reference pixels alone weren't enough to meet NIRSpec's 6 e⁻ noise requirement. Solution: the IRS2 readout mode interleaves 4 reference pixels for every 16 science pixels during clocking. This distributed reference data is processed in Fourier space using frequency-dependent optimal weights, then subtracted. The result: correlated noise suppressed at every spatial frequency, achieving the seemingly impossible 6 e⁻ total noise floor.

Step 3: Dark current subtraction (ground processing)

Even with no light, detectors generate a small "dark current" — thermal electrons that mimic signal. At 37 K, this is tiny (~0.003 electrons/sec/pixel for NIRCam) but non-zero. Calibration exposures taken with the shutter closed provide a dark frame that is subtracted from every science image.

Step 4: Flat-field correction

No detector is perfectly uniform. Some pixels are 2% more sensitive, others 3% less. A "flat field" — an image of a uniform light source — maps these pixel-to-pixel variations. Dividing the science image by the flat field corrects for them.

Accuracy target: <0.5% photometric error pixel-to-pixel. Achieving this requires calibrating not just sensitivity but also intra-pixel response variations (the sensitivity varies even WITHIN a single pixel depending on where the photon lands).

Step 5: Astrometric calibration and distortion correction

The optical path introduces geometric distortions — straight lines in the sky appear slightly curved on the detector. A distortion model (polynomial coefficients derived from observations of known star fields) maps each detector pixel to its true sky position. This correction must be accurate to ~5 milliarcseconds — the angle subtended by a 1-cent coin at 800 km distance.

Step 6: Background subtraction

Even in "empty" space, there's a background glow: zodiacal light (sunlight scattered by interplanetary dust), unresolved faint galaxies, and thermal emission from the telescope itself. This background must be modelled and subtracted to reveal the faintest science targets.

Step 7: Drizzling and mosaicking

JWST takes multiple slightly-offset exposures (dithers) of the same field. The "drizzle" algorithm combines these sub-pixel-shifted images to produce a final image with resolution better than any single exposure — recovering spatial information between pixel boundaries.

For large mosaics (like the JADES deep field), dozens of pointings are stitched together, with overlapping regions used to cross-calibrate brightness levels.

Drizzling in everyday terms: Imagine photographing a mosaic floor with a camera whose pixels are bigger than the tiles. Each photo is blurry. But if you take 20 photos, each shifted by half a tile width, you can reconstruct the tile pattern at a resolution your camera "shouldn't" be able to achieve. JWST does this with starlight.

The Hardest DSP Problem: Exoplanet Transit Spectroscopy

Extracting 50 ppm signals from systematic noise

The challenge

Detecting an exoplanet's atmosphere requires measuring brightness changes of 50–200 parts per million over several hours. The dominant noise source is NOT photon noise — it's instrumental systematics:

• Pointing jitter: As the telescope drifts slightly (milliarcseconds), the star image moves across pixels with different sensitivities, creating fake brightness variations of ~100 ppm.

• Detector settling: After slewing to a new target, detector behaviour is unstable for ~30–60 minutes ("charge trapping" and "persistence" from previous bright exposures).

• 1/f noise: Low-frequency drift in the amplifier electronics creates correlated noise on timescales of minutes to hours — exactly the timescale of a transit.

• Thermal breathing: Tiny temperature variations in the optics cause focus changes that redistribute light between pixels.

How they solve it

Decorrelation models: Measured systematics (telescope position, detector temperature, time since slew) are used as regressors in a linear model. The transit signal is fit simultaneously with the systematic model — essentially asking: "what's the simplest transit shape that explains the residuals after removing known instrumental effects?"

Gaussian processes: For correlated noise that can't be tied to a known physical parameter, Gaussian process regression provides a flexible model of the noise structure without assuming a specific functional form. It's a Bayesian approach to saying "I don't know what's causing this wiggle, but I can characterise its correlation structure."

Pixel-level decorrelation: Instead of summing all light from a star into one number (aperture photometry), each pixel's time-series is analysed independently. This allows sub-pixel sensitivity variations to be modelled and removed.

Allan variance analysis: Used to quantify how noise averages down with integration time. If noise is white (uncorrelated), it should decrease as 1/√t. Departures from this reveal systematic floors — the point beyond which integrating longer doesn't help.

Transit depth ≈ (Rplanet / Rstar)² ≈ (0.1 R☉ / 1.0 R☉)² = 1% for a Jupiter
Atmospheric signal ≈ 2 × H × Rp / Rstar² ≈ 50–200 ppm
The SNR reality: A single transit observation might have SNR ≈ 3 per spectral channel. That's useless individually — you can't distinguish signal from noise. But stack 10 transit observations and you get SNR ≈ 10. JWST's transit programmes often observe 5–20 transits of the same planet, each 3–8 hours long. A single definitive exoplanet atmosphere measurement can require 100+ hours of telescope time.

On-board vs Ground Processing

What happens on-board (RAD750)

• Frame averaging (co-adding groups of reads to reduce data volume)
• Lossless data compression (~2:1 ratio using a Rice-type algorithm)
• Data packetisation for downlink
• Instrument health monitoring and fault protection
• Guide star acquisition and fine guidance tracking (real-time closed loop at 16 Hz)

The flight software does NOT do science processing. No cosmic ray rejection, no flat-fielding, no source detection. All science lives on the ground.

What happens on the ground (STScI pipeline)

The full pipeline has three stages:

Stage 1 (detector-level): Ramp fitting, saturation flagging, dark subtraction, linearity correction, reference pixel subtraction → produces "rate images" (counts/second per pixel).

Stage 2 (calibration): Flat-field, photometric calibration, WCS assignment, background subtraction → produces calibrated 2D images or extracted spectra.

Stage 3 (combination): Drizzling, mosaicking, source detection, spectral extraction → produces science-ready products.

Total processing for a typical deep-field observation: ~2–8 hours of compute time on the STScI cluster. The pipeline is open-source Python (jwst package), meaning any astronomer can re-process the raw data with custom parameters.

Data lifecycle: Raw photons hit the detector → accumulate as charge (analogue) → sampled as ADU counts (16-bit digital) → compressed and transmitted → decompressed at STScI → pipeline produces calibrated science images → astronomers extract measurements → papers written → textbooks rewritten. The chain from photon to publication typically takes 3–12 months.

Unanswered Questions / Active DSP Research

  • Can machine learning models outperform traditional pipeline steps for cosmic ray rejection and noise characterisation?
  • The 1/f noise floor currently limits some science cases. Can novel readout schemes (e.g., IPC correction, improved reference pixel strategies) push it lower?
  • As JWST ages, detector degradation (hot pixels, increased dark current) will require adaptive calibration. How will the pipeline evolve?
  • Future telescopes (HWO) will need 10× better stability for Earth-like exoplanet detection (~10 ppb). What DSP innovations are needed?

The information-theoretic limit: how faint can we go?

There's a fascinating fundamental question buried in JWST's DSP: what's the theoretical limit of information extractable from these data?

Consider: JWST's deep fields detect galaxies at magnitude ~31 — that's about 1 photon per 500 seconds per pixel. At this level, you're not doing traditional imaging — you're doing photon counting statistics. The "image" is really a probability distribution reconstructed from Poisson events.

Shannon's channel capacity theorem tells us that the information in a signal is bounded by the number of independent measurements × the SNR. For JWST's deepest observations, we're approaching ~1 bit of information per pixel per hour. The images we see are statistical reconstructions — not photographs in any traditional sense. They're more like medical CT scans built from sparse projections.

Future space telescopes may push to magnitude 34+ — where you're measuring ~1 photon per hour per pixel. At that point, the detector must be so stable that you can trust it hasn't drifted over the 100-hour integration needed to build up a signal. The DSP challenge transitions from "filtering noise" to "maintaining calibration stability over absurdly long timescales." This is where JWST's lessons will directly inform HWO's design.

The Shannon limit framing is illustrative, not rigorous — real astronomical signals have spatial and spectral correlations that provide additional information beyond single-pixel statistics. But the core insight holds: JWST is operating at the edge of what's physically possible with current detector technology.
