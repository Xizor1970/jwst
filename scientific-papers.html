The steepest publication curve of any space mission

According to STScI's bibliometric analysis, "the rapid rise of papers that analyse JWST data is steeper than for any other mission hosted by MAST." For context: Hubble didn't reach 1,000 papers per year until 15 years into its mission. JWST crossed that threshold in just its third full year of operations.

The Early Release Science (ERS) programmes were extraordinarily productive — a median of 18 papers and 586 citations per programme. These deliberately open-access datasets allowed the entire community to learn the telescope's capabilities simultaneously, creating a wave of parallel discovery.

Publication velocity: At 85 papers per month, JWST produces roughly 3 new peer-reviewed scientific discoveries every single day — weekends and holidays included. Each one has passed through months of analysis, peer review, and revision. The telescope generates data faster than the global astronomical community can fully analyse it. Archival discoveries from JWST data will continue for decades.

Where the Science Lands

Breakdown by topic

Galaxies & cosmology (~58%): The dominant science area — early galaxy formation, luminosity functions, cosmic star formation history, galaxy morphology, and large-scale structure. This is what JWST was primarily designed for.

Stellar physics (~13%): Star formation, stellar populations, resolved stars in nearby galaxies, supernova remnants, planetary nebulae.

Exoplanets & planet formation (~8%): Atmospheric characterisation, protoplanetary disks, transit spectroscopy. Small percentage but highest public impact.

Supermassive black holes & AGN (~3.5%): The "Little Red Dots," early quasars, direct collapse candidates. Growing rapidly.

Solar system (~2%): Jupiter, Saturn, ice giants, ocean moons, asteroids. Small but high-impact discoveries.

Instrumentation & methods (~15%): Calibration papers, pipeline descriptions, novel techniques.

By instrument

NIRCam: 1,310 papers — the workhorse. Deep imaging, galaxy surveys, photometric redshifts.
NIRSpec: 908 papers — the spectroscopic powerhouse. Redshift confirmation, atmospheric chemistry, emission lines.
MIRI: 752 papers — mid-infrared. Dust emission, AGN, cool objects, solar system.
NIRISS: 195 papers — slitless spectroscopy and aperture masking. Exoplanet transits, wide-field spectra.

The knowledge multiplier: JWST cost $9.7 billion. At 2,236 papers, that's ~$4.3 million per paper — expensive, but each paper spawns further research, graduate theses, new proposals, instrument development, and textbook revisions. The Hubble Space Telescope has generated >19,000 papers over 35 years and is estimated to have returned $2.50 in economic value for every $1 invested. JWST's trajectory suggests it will exceed Hubble's total output in less than half the time.

What's striking about this list

  • Papers #5–#13 are ALL about "impossibly early galaxies" — this is the dominant discovery theme of JWST's first years.
  • Papers #4 and #14–#18 are the WASP-39b atmosphere suite — 5 papers in Nature from a single planet, each using a different JWST instrument. Unprecedented coordination.
  • The #1 most-cited paper is about the telescope's performance, not a science result — because every other JWST paper cites it to describe the instrument.
  • Black hole papers (#30, #36–#40) are newer and climbing fast — the "Little Red Dots" are likely to dominate the next generation of top-cited papers.

What will JWST's legacy look like in 2040?

If the current trajectory holds (doubling every ~2 years then plateauing as Hubble did), JWST will produce 10,000–15,000 papers over a 20-year mission — likely surpassing Hubble's 35-year total of 19,000+ in less than half the time.

But raw numbers miss the point. The papers that matter most are the ones that change paradigms — and JWST already has several candidates: the "impossible early galaxies" that may require new physics, the first potential biosignature (K2-18b), and the discovery that black holes may have formed before their host galaxies. Any one of these could become a Nobel Prize discovery if confirmed by follow-up observations.

The most important JWST paper probably hasn't been written yet. Every major telescope's greatest discovery was something it wasn't designed to find.
