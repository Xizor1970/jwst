The Most Competitive Grant in Science

~6× more requests than available time. A 16% success rate. Higher rejection than Harvard admissions.

The numbers

JWST observes for approximately 8,760 hours per year (it never sleeps). After subtracting calibration, slewing time, and engineering, about 6,000 hours are available for science.

In Cycle 1 (first year), astronomers submitted proposals requesting over 38,000 hours — about 6.3× more than available. Only ~16% of proposals were accepted. By Cycle 3, demand remained at ~5.4× oversubscription. The competition is fierce, international, and blind-reviewed.

Each hour of JWST time "costs" roughly $150,000 (total mission cost ÷ total observing hours over the mission). This makes it among the most expensive scientific instruments per hour of operation in history.

The economics of scarcity: 6,000 hours per year, split among ~1,000 astronomers and teams worldwide, for science questions spanning all of astrophysics — from solar system moons to the most distant galaxies. A typical deep-field observation takes 50–100 hours. A transit spectroscopy programme might need 200+ hours across multiple planets. The telescope is booked years in advance, with every hour fought over.

How the proposal process works

1. Call for Proposals: STScI (Space Telescope Science Institute, Baltimore) issues an annual call with available instrument modes, time allocation, and scientific priorities.

2. Submission: Astronomers submit proposals detailing: scientific justification, technical feasibility, observation strategy, expected results, and team qualifications. A typical proposal is 4–6 pages of dense scientific argument.

3. Dual-anonymous review: Since Cycle 1, JWST uses dual-anonymous peer review (DAPR) — reviewers don't know who submitted the proposal, and proposers don't know who reviews them. This was specifically implemented to reduce bias against early-career researchers, women, and scientists at smaller institutions. (Studies showed that named review favoured senior scientists at prestigious universities.)

4. Panel review: Expert panels (8–12 scientists each) read, discuss, and rank proposals within their science category. Panels are balanced by career stage, gender, and institution type.

5. Time Allocation Committee (TAC): A senior committee adjudicates between categories and selects the final programme.

6. Scheduling: Accepted programmes are scheduled by a complex optimisation algorithm that must satisfy pointing constraints, thermal limits, guide star availability, and data volume budgets.

Proposal categories

GO (General Observer): The main competitive pool. Any astronomer worldwide can propose. ~70% of time.

GTO (Guaranteed Time Observations): Reserved for instrument teams who built JWST's hardware — their "payment" for decades of development work. ~15% of time in early cycles, decreasing over time.

DD (Director's Discretionary): Small reserve for time-critical targets (supernovae, gravitational wave counterparts, unexpected discoveries). The STScI director allocates this in near-real-time.

ERS (Early Release Science): First-cycle programmes designed to demonstrate capabilities and produce open-access data quickly, enabling the community to learn how to use JWST. Data public immediately (no proprietary period).

Open data policy

After a 12-month exclusive access period, all JWST data becomes public. Any astronomer — or interested citizen — can download the raw data from the Mikulski Archive for Space Telescopes (MAST) and process it themselves. Many citizen scientists and amateur astronomers have done exactly this, sometimes producing results that rival professional teams.

Some programmes (ERS, DD, and some GO) have zero exclusive access — data is public immediately upon downlink. This reflects a philosophical commitment: the telescope belongs to humanity, not to any individual team.

The JWST economy: Think of JWST time like prime real estate in a city with no room to expand. Demand permanently exceeds supply. The "currency" is scientific merit (judged by peers). The "rent" is measured in hours. And unlike real estate, you can't buy more — only compete. A brilliant proposal from a PhD student at a small university in India has the same shot (via DAPR) as one from a Nobel laureate at Harvard. That's the design intent.

Tensions in the System

  • Should JWST allocate more time to risky "moonshot" proposals, or prioritise safe, incremental science?
  • The dual-anonymous system prevents bias — but also prevents recognising that some teams have unique expertise or access to complementary data. Is the tradeoff worth it?
  • Should "science influencers" who publicise results broadly (increasing public support for astronomy funding) get more time than those who publish only in journals?
  • As AI tools enable faster proposal writing, will review panels be overwhelmed by volume? Should there be limits on proposals per astronomer?
